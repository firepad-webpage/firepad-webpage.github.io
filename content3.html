Relevant Content for Abstract Preparation

Motivation of the Study

Traditional secret sharing ensures that a secret can only be reconstructed by an authorized set of shares, but this assumes an adversary cannot eventually obtain such a set. This assumption is unrealistic over long periods with potential multiple breaches [p.1]. The study introduces secret sharing with certified deletion, leveraging quantum mechanics to allow users to verify the deletion of shares, ensuring privacy even if an adversary later obtains an authorized set [p.1].

Background

Certified deletion relies on quantum mechanics, where destructive measurements can erase useful information from quantum shares. The concept was first introduced by Broadbent and Islam (2020) for one-time-pad encryption. Bartusek and Khurana (2023) extended it to 2-out-of-2 secret sharing, but general secret sharing structures remained unaddressed [p.2].

Results

Two Security Definitions

No-Signaling Security: Ensures privacy if non-communicating adversaries delete enough shares, even if the remaining shares form an authorized set [p.3].

Adaptive Security: Protects against adversaries that adaptively corrupt and delete shares, provided the undeleted corrupted shares remain unauthorized [p.3].

Construction of Certified Deletion Schemes

General Secret Sharing Scheme: A general secret sharing scheme with no-signaling certified deletion for any monotone access structure, using Bartusek and Khurana’s 2-out-of-2 scheme as a building block [p.4].

Threshold Secret Sharing Scheme: A threshold secret sharing scheme with adaptive certified deletion, constructed from scratch using new technical ideas, including generalized XOR extractors and polynomial interpolation as a randomness extractor [p.5].

High-Rate Seedless Extractors

Developed new seedless extractors for quantum sources of entropy, improving the rate of extraction and enabling efficient certified deletion. These extractors work for both qubit and qudit registers and extend to finite fields, reducing the need for entanglement [p.6].

Efficient Share Deletion and Verification

Share deletion involves measuring in the Fourier basis and outputting the result as a deletion certificate, verifiable using pre-defined check positions in each share [p.7].

Proof of Security

Security proofs demonstrate negligible trace distance between the adversary’s view of different secrets after certified deletion, even under adaptive attacks. This involves a hybrid argument and cut-and-choose techniques to ensure that polynomial interpolation acts as an effective randomness extractor [p.8].

Open Problems

The authors highlight future directions, including extending adaptive certified deletion to general access structures, unifying no-signaling and adaptive security, achieving publicly verifiable deletion certificates, and exploring other threshold primitives like threshold signatures [p.12].
